Air Pollution and Mortality in California

The average adult at rest inhales and exhales about 7 or 8 liters of air per minute (about 0.25 cubic feet) and 11,000 liters of air (388 cubic feet) in a day. Given the large volume of air we breathe daily it is no surprise that air quality will impact the quality of health. In the following visualization we will be focusing on four major air pollutants nitrogen dioxide (NO2), sulphur dioxide (SO2), carbon monoxide (CO) and ground level ozone (O3) and four diseases respiratory disease, heart disease, stroke and cancer, that are scientifically known to be associated with bad air quality. The concentrations of air pollutants in the air are often influenced by seasonal changes so these measures have been aggregated by month and year to capture these differences. O3 is more prevalent when there is more sunlight so the summer months will have higher levels of O3. The other pollutants NO2, SO2, and CO are more prevalent with higher humidity levels so they are more prevalent in the winter months. Most of the pollutants NO2, SO2, and O3 affect respiratory diseases due to lung irritation. CO affects the ability of oxygen to bind to hemoglobin in blood cells which is vital to carrying oxygen throughout the body and negatively impacts both respiratory and cardiovascular systems of the body. Yearly mortality rates were derived from the number of deaths normalized by the population counts by city and are expressed per 100,000 people by yearly basis.

Pollution

Data was obtained from the Environmental Protection Agency which collects data about various pollutants through the use of air monitors placed in a variety of locations. Pollutant measures are expressed in terms of Air Quality Index (AQI), which are directly related to health outcomes. The original data used click here. For more information about air pollutants click here. For more information about AQI measures click here.

Mortality

Data about mortality rates was obtained through the California Department of Public Health leading causes of death dataset. Mortality rates were derived by using population denominators for various years derived from the American Community Survey and Census data.
